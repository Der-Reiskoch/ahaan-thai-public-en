Understand Spellings of Thai Dishes

Why some dishes are spelled so differently

Table of contents
  • RTGS and IPA
  • Fried Suitcase
  • Some Examples
  • Video from Pailin

Have you ever wondered why the names of Thai dishes are spelled so differently? With this short article, I want to bring some clarity to the various spellings of Thai dishes on my blog. I hope these few short paragraphs shed some light on the confusion surrounding Thai food names, and perhaps you can forgive me for the occasional linguistic slip-up.

RTGS and IPA

I myself cannot read or write Thai, and I assume the same goes for you, so we rely on transcriptions of Thai script into the Latin alphabet we’re familiar with. There is actually a standard system for this, the Royal Thai General System of Transcription (RTGS), yet despite this, there are sometimes very different spellings for individual dishes.

One reason for this is certainly the different target languages, as RTGS first transcribes Thai only into the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA) and not directly into another language. The IPA in turn describes different languages such as German and English in different ways. For example, the Thai word for pork ( หมู ) becomes “Moo” in English transcription, while the German version is more like “Mhu”. What you can also often observe is that German transcriptions use a “G” where English spellings use a “K”.

Now, English-language recipes for Thai dishes have a much wider reach on the internet compared to German ones. Thus, you find the former much more quickly, which gives English spellings a certain dominance. Furthermore, many German-language sites have adopted these English transcriptions, giving them even greater distribution.

When I started recreating recipes and publishing them on my blog, I almost automatically relied mostly on English-language sources or German-language sources with English transcription. And the cooking classes I attended in Thailand - except for one - were also conducted in English. As a result, many English spellings have become established in my head and on my blog, but not consistently.

Fried Suitcase

Since I want to ensure a consistent naming of dishes at least within my blog, I deliberately kept some “incorrect” spellings until recently, even though I knew better by then. A very good example of this was one of my favorite dishes ผัดกะเพรา , which I always called “Pad Kra Pao”, which in this spelling actually describes a fried suitcase ( ผัดกระเป๋า ) in Thai and not the well-known dish with Holy Thai Basil that it’s actually about. A “more correct” spelling is actually Pad Ga Prau, which I now use.
